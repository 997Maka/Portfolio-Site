My Process

Discover

I started off my journey by learning more about the mobile payments industry. I looked into existing mobile payments and financial apps and conducted a competitive analysis on two major payment apps, PayPal and Venmo, to gain insight into their strengths and weaknesses. I also looked up app reviews from existing users to find out if there are any pain points in these apps from an UX point of view. I used these findings to compose my problem statement and to identify opportunities for my own app.

Next, I conducted user research through a survey and interviews to gain a deeper understanding of people's financial behaviours and habits and how they might use the app.

I documented and sorted my research findings into four categories: behaviors/attitudes, needs/goals, frustrations, quotes. I then sorted the data into an affinity map to help organize the information and identify themes and insights.

Define

My user research participants inspired me in creating my user personas. I created four different personas because I wanted to showcase a range of potential users from different ages and background with whom I can emphasize with and who could all benefit from my app. The personas and their needs helped me narrow down the main functions I would be building into my app so that I can maximize its usability.

I then more thoroughly brainstormed and conceptualized the paths that users will take to navigate the app and accomplish tasks. User flows helped me organize and visualize my thoughts.

Develope

Before moving onto wireframing, I conducted a card sorting exercise with participants to finalize my sitemap.

I started my wireframing process with low-fidelity sketches, then moved onto mid-fidelity wireframes. With each new version, I reviewed my designs and asked myself questions such as:

  • Is there a better or easier way to perform this task?
  • Is this design too confusing?
  • Does this content belong on this page?

I had very clear objectives to guide my design decision-making:

  • Do not overcrowd frames with unnecessary content
  • Keep a clean look by best leveraging the use of white space
  • Stay consistent on design format, text, and styles

It took many rounds of iteration to improve my design and offer a better experience to users.

Deliver

I tested my mid-fidelity prototype in usability tests with 6 participants. Through user testing, I gained insights on the parts of my app that worked well and flowed smoothly and also weaknesses and friction points that I needed to address. It highlighted to me the main areas that required more thinking and iteration.

Lessons Learned

Don’t easily make assumptions

I discovered through my user testing sessions that sometimes my assumptions, which guide my design choices, can be entirely inaccurate and not in line with user’s thought processes at all. Though I may still make assumptions in the future, I now have lower expectations to achieve a desired result with prototypes, but instead let users show me where I can learn and iterate. Most importantly, it has highlighted to me the importance of remaining empathetithroughout the entire design process.

Don’t obsess over perfection

The more I developed my wireframes and prototypes, the more I wanted to perfect every single flow, detail and feature and add more details so that my app can be even more useful. I’ve learned that it is unrealistic to achieve a perfect product and grounded myself by keeping the project goals and requirements in mind. I understood that this was a continuous learning process and that I can always apply what was left out this time to future projects.

Iterate, Iterate, then Iterate more!

Each time I revisited my design, I would notice something new and could identify areas where I can improve. I really enjoyed the iteration process because I could apply my problem-solving skills in a creative way and the more I iterated, the more I felt confident in my product and design choices. I also felt more confident in myself because I was growing as a designer.

Accept criticism but also be confident in design choices

I sought and received feedback and design critiques from participants and fellow designers throughout the iteration process. While I accepted and made changes based on some of the feedback, I also disagreed with some of them because they did not align with my design objectives. I learned to be ok with rejecting certain critiques and staying confident in maintaining and backing up my design decisions.
